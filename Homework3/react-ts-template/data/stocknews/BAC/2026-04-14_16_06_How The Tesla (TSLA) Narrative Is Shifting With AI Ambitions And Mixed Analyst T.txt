Tesla’s updated analyst narrative now incorporates a slightly lower core fair value estimate, moving from US$421.27 to US$415.30. This reflects a modest reset in price targets. That change mirrors what you are seeing across Street research, where some firms are lifting targets and others are trimming them as they reassess execution risks, capital needs and how much weight to give potential AI and energy upside. Read on to see what is driving these views and how you can keep track of the story as it evolves. Analyst Price Targets don't always capture the full story. BofA reinstated Tesla with a Buy rating and a US$460 price target, highlighting the company as a leader in consumer autonomy and potential robotaxi services, and tying a large part of its thesis to Tesla's ability to scale AI enabled mobility. Mizuho raised its Tesla price target by US$10, and Morgan Stanley has discussed Tesla Energy and solar as potential value drivers, with Morgan Stanley outlining scenarios in which solar could add materially to the energy storage business. UBS has raised its Tesla price target in prior work and continues to publish views on vehicle deliveries and autonomy, which many investors watch as markers for execution on both EV and self driving roadmaps. Several firms, including Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, Canaccord, Truist, Baird and JPMorgan, have trimmed Tesla price targets in recent months, citing factors such as capital intensity, execution risk and reassessment of AI and energy upside in their rationale. Wedbush, Stifel and William Blair have highlighted underwhelming Q1 deliveries and energy storage results as constraints on the near term story, while Barclays has flagged that Tesla's push into physical AI could be costly, adding another layer of execution risk for investors to monitor. Tesla reported first quarter 2026 operating metrics, with production of over 408,000 vehicles, deliveries of over 358,000 vehicles and 8.8 GWh of energy storage products deployed across its auto and energy businesses. Reuters reports that Tesla plans to develop a smaller, cheaper EV and has contacted suppliers, pointing to potential expansion of the lineup beyond current higher priced models. SpaceX and Tesla plan to build two advanced chip factories at a large Austin facility, one for vehicle and Optimus robot chips and another for AI data centers in space, linking Tesla's AI and robotics efforts with manufacturing. Pilot Travel Centers agreed with Tesla to install Semi Chargers at select U.S. truck stops, with initial sites planned along major freight corridors and capacity of up to 1.2 megawatts per stall.
